CatchScapeFS

Assessment of farming systems sustainability
in Northern Thailand

Chakrit Potchanasin (Hohenheim Univ.) & Nicolas Becu (CNRS)

The CatchScapeFS model was developed in order to evaluate the sustainability of farming systems in the study area of Bor Krai village, Mae Hong Son province, Thailand. Integrated modelling of the present study provides new opportunities to evaluate and monitor sustainability situation and deliver information for policy development. The sustainability of farming systems in the study area is under stress due to tension on resource use, population growth, land limitation, and increasing market options. In long run, this situation may result in food insecurity, shift of farming systems to market orientation, and increasing environmental problems e.g. soil erosion and land degradation.

The model is composed of two principle components: biophysical and socioeconomic.  The biophysical component is based on CatchScape3 model (Becu et al., 2003) which consists of biophysical sub-models: a hydrological model, a crop model, a water balance model, and a soil erosion model embedded in the study area’s landscape model which is divided into spatial grid cells representing agricultural plots of one rai (0.16 ha) (see beside the study area representation in Cormas).

Socioeconomic component is composed of farm household agents and the other social elements determined based on farming system approach (Potchanasin et al., 2008). Decision making processes are structured following behavior heuristics presented in decision tree diagram. This approach has been selected among an alternative option, optimization approach. The approach has been selected with concerning about flexibility to involve qualitative factors, considering search cost to model agents’ bounded rationality, advancing in communication with stakeholders and advancing to integrate agents’ ability to perceive environment and communication among agent society (Potchanasin, C. 2008).

To generate farm agents, cluster analysis and qualitative analysis were applied together with Monte Carlo techniques (figure). This analysis allowed defining a consistent characteristic distribution between the agent population and the farm samples. Each simulation time step corresponds to a 10 day time interval and consists of six phases (biophysical dynamics, farm household activities, socioeconomic dynamics, information exchanges and result arrangements). The farm household activities consist of eight sub-phases: knowledge base updating, household resource updating, cropping activities, household activities, harvesting, selling farm products, financial activities and livestock activities. The model validation was conducted through social validation (diagram elicitation approach) and statistic data comparison validation.

The model simulations deliver sustainability evaluation results. Sustainability of the study area was evaluated at two level, household and village level,  covering 15 year starting by 2003 until 2017 (Potchanasin et al., 2008). At household level, sustainability was assessed based on farm agents’ performance which this is why agent-based modelling is chosen. At village level, sustainability was assessed through Sustainability index and Performance index which these enhance determination of sustainability issue and comparison across defined indicators (figure, figure).
